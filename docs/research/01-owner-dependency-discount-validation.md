## Structural Valuation Adjustments for Key Person Risk and Owner Dependency in Small-Market Private Enterprise Transactions

The valuation of small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) with transaction values ranging from $500,000 to $10,000,000 occupies a specialized niche within the private capital markets, characterized by an acute sensitivity to human capital risks. Within this segment, the most significant non-market driver of valuation volatility is owner dependency, often conceptualized by practitioners and academics as "key man risk" or the "hub-and-spoke" operational model. This condition refers to a state where the business's revenue generation, operational continuity, and strategic viability are fundamentally anchored to the individual efforts, relationships, or specialized knowledge of the principal owner. Professional valuation standards and transaction data from prominent industry bodies confirm that when a business is perceived as an extension of the individual rather than a transferable institution, it suffers a material reduction in its enterprise value. This report provides an exhaustive quantification of these valuation discounts, synthesizing data from peer-reviewed academic research, the International Business Brokers Association (IBBA), BizBuySell, the Exit Planning Institute (EPI), and the Pepperdine Private Capital Markets Survey.

## Conceptual Framework and Definitions of Management Dependency Risk

To quantify the financial impact of owner dependency, it is first necessary to establish the technical definitions used by valuation professionals and M&A advisors. The International Business Brokers Association (IBBA) and M&A Source frequently describe this risk as the lack of "transferable value," which represents the portion of the business's economic benefit that can be reliably passed to a successor.1 The "hub-and-spoke" model is a common industry term for a business structure where the owner acts as the central hub through which all operational, strategic, and customer-related decisions must pass.3 In such organizations, the owner typically oversees most teams and key functions directly rather than working through a delegated management layer, remains the primary point of contact for major clients, and controls essential administrative or financial recordkeeping.5

Professional valuation practitioners, including firms like Website Closers, define owner dependence as a condition where a company "cannot reliably sustain its historical performance without the direct management of the owner".5 This dependency triggers two primary valuation adjustments: a key person risk discount and a discount for lack of marketability (DLOM). The key person risk adjustment reflects the heightened probability of financial loss following the owner's departure, while the DLOM addresses the reduced pool of potential buyers willing to acquire a high-risk, owner-centric operation.5

Academic and practitioner literature further bifurcates this risk through the lens of "Personal Goodwill" versus "Enterprise Goodwill." Personal goodwill consists of the intangible value arising from the owner's unique reputation, skills, and relationships—assets that are legally and practically difficult to convey to a buyer.6 Enterprise goodwill, conversely, is the value inherent in the company's systems, brand, and institutionalized processes. When a significant portion of a firm's value is categorized as personal goodwill, it results in a direct "haircut" to the purchase price, as sophisticated buyers typically refuse to pay for assets that exit the business alongside the seller.9

## Quantitative Benchmarks for Owner Dependency Multiples and Value Reductions

The most visible manifestation of owner dependency in the SME market is multiple compression. In the lower middle market (generally defined as businesses with values above $2 million), valuation is typically expressed as a multiple of EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization). In the "Main Street" market (values below $2 million), the standard metric is Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE). Research indicates that businesses possessing robust operational systems and low owner dependency command premium multiples, while those with high dependency face severe reductions.

### Practitioner Data on Multiple Compression

According to research published by SE-Adv, "Founder-dependent businesses often receive valuations 30-50% below market comparables".3 The firm provides a specific comparative range: "While independent businesses in the lower middle market sell for 7-8x EBITDA, founder-dependent companies struggle to achieve 3-4x multiples".3 This 50% reduction in the valuation multiple represents the market's pricing of transition risk and the cost required to hire a replacement management team to institutionalize the owner's roles.

Further support for these ranges is provided by Website Closers, which highlights that a company capable of operating independently might be worth $3.5 million to $4 million, whereas the same business would be worth only $1.5 million to $2 million if the owner were considered essential.5 Their data establishes a clear "transferability premium," noting that "businesses that run themselves" trade at 7 to 8 times yearly profits, while "businesses that depend on the owner" trade at only 3 to 4 times.5 This finding is consistent across the SME spectrum, suggesting that nearly half of a business's potential value can be eroded by the failure to decentralize management.

### IBBA and M&A Source Market Pulse Findings

The International Business Brokers Association (IBBA), in conjunction with M&A Source, tracks median multiples through their quarterly "Market Pulse" report. These reports serve as the national benchmark for SME valuations. In the "Market Pulse Q4 2022 Executive Summary," advisors reported that median SDE multiples for businesses under $500,000 were 2.0x, while businesses in the $5 million to $50 million range traded at 4.1x EBITDA—a notable drop from the peak of 6.8x seen in 2021.11

The sensitivity of these multiples to management risk is underscored by specific practitioner findings within the IBBA network. For instance, research from Exit Lab HVAC, citing the IBBA "Market Pulse Report Q4 2025," notes that "reducing owner dependency can add 0.5x to 1.0x to your EBITDA multiple".12 On a business generating $1 million in EBITDA, this operational improvement alone translates to a value increase of $500,000 to $1,000,000, illustrating the high cost of the "owner-as-operator" model.

## Professional Valuation Theory: The Shannon Pratt Key Person Discount

In the discipline of formal business appraisal, Shannon Pratt is recognized as a preeminent authority on the valuation of closely held firms. His work, including "The Lawyer's Business Valuation Handbook" and "Cost of Capital Estimation and Applications," provides the theoretical foundation for applying discounts at the entity level.14 Pratt suggests that a specific key person risk discount should be applied when a business is dependent on one or a few key individuals whose absence would materially affect operations.17

The range established by Pratt for a key person discount is generally 10% to 25%.5 While some practitioners apply higher "haircuts" in transactional contexts, Pratt's range is frequently cited in litigation and formal appraisals to reflect the "detriment to value" between a business with an institutionalized leadership structure and one with a single point of failure. This discount is often applied after the initial marketable indication of value has been determined, functioning as a "bottom-line" adjustment to the fair market value of the interest.

## Integration of Key Person Risk into the Discount Rate

While some appraisers apply a flat percentage discount to the final value, others incorporate owner dependency risk into the discount rate used in the Income Approach. Under the "Build-Up Method," a Company-Specific Risk Premium (CSRP) is added to the risk-free rate, the equity risk premium, and the size premium. Practitioners such as David Goodman have proposed using the Multi-Attribute Utility Model (MAUT) to derive a quantifiable CSRP.18

Research into "company-specific risk" demonstrates that owner dependency can contribute significantly to the required rate of return. Goodman's research provides a case study for a technical SME ("Analog Electronics") where management-related risks—including the owner being the "primary technical guru" and the lack of a technical succession plan—were quantified.18 By assessing these attributes through a Multi-Attribute Utility Model and Monte Carlo simulation, the researcher concluded that a 7% CSRP was appropriate, with a 95% certainty range of 6% to 8%.18 Small business "rules of thumb" frequently add 3% to 7% to the discount rate specifically to account for owner dependence and customer concentration.20

## The Multi-Attribute Utility Model (MUM) for Personal Goodwill Allocation

One of the most robust methods for quantifying owner dependency in a way that withstands legal and tax scrutiny is the Multi-Attribute Utility Model (MUM), originally introduced by David Wood, CPA.21 This model is specifically designed to distinguish "Personal Goodwill" from "Enterprise Goodwill".9 In SME transactions, identifying the percentage of value tied to the owner's persona is critical, as most buyers will only pay for enterprise goodwill—the value that survives the owner's exit.9

### The Mechanics of MUM Allocation

The MUM model functions by defining specific attributes of both personal and enterprise goodwill and assessing their importance and existence within the subject company.9 Personal goodwill attributes include the professional's reputation for judgment, skill, and knowledge, as well as the owner's age, health, and personal relationships with referral sources.22 Enterprise goodwill attributes include the company's systems, numerous offices, strategic location, and established brand name.9

The model follows a structured seven-step process:

1. Define the objective (to allocate value between personal and enterprise goodwill).
2. Establish alternatives (expressed as percentage ranges, such as 0-20%, 20-40%, etc.).
3. Define the specific attributes.
4. Assess the importance utility (IU) and existence utility (EU) of each attribute.
5. Aggregate the multiplicative utility.
6. Fit the results to the alternatives using a sensitivity analysis.
7. Express the opinion as a final percentage.22

### Empirical Findings using the MUM Model

In practical applications, the MUM model often reveals that in owner-dependent SMEs, a vast majority of the intangible value is non-transferable. For example, a case study reported by the Illinois State Bar Association detailed a valuation where the multiplicative utility of personal goodwill was calculated to be 76.1%, leading to a rounded conclusion that 70% of the company's total goodwill was personal.22

This 70% allocation represents a theoretical "ceiling" for the discount; if 70% of the intangible value is tied to the owner and cannot be transferred, a buyer would fundamentally discount the total purchase price by a similar margin if no transition plan or management replacement were possible. This model provides a quantifiable, court-accepted method for showing how the "hub-and-spoke" model directly diminishes enterprise value.

## Normalization of Earnings and the "Hidden" Management Replacement Cost

Before a multiple or discount rate can be applied, the "normalized" earnings of the SME must be determined. In owner-dependent businesses, the reported financial statements often do not reflect the true cost of operating the business without the owner's labor. Practitioners at Sofer Advisors highlight that normalization commonly adds 15% to 40% to reported EBITDA in closely held businesses where owner compensation exceeds a market replacement salary.13

However, in many small SMEs ($500k-$5M), the owner is often underpaid relative to the value of the work they perform (e.g., acting as CEO, sales manager, and lead engineer simultaneously). In these instances, the "normalization" process requires subtracting a market-rate management replacement salary from the EBITDA. For an SME generating $500,000 in EBITDA where the owner performs the work of two managers worth $150,000 each, the "Transferable EBITDA" falls to $200,000.

This reduction in the earnings base, coupled with the multiple compression described earlier, creates a compounding effect on the valuation discount:

- **Earnings Hit:** Normalized EBITDA is reduced by the cost of replacing the "key man."
- **Multiple Hit:** The multiple is reduced because of the risk that the replacement will be less effective than the founder.

According to the Pepperdine Private Capital Markets Report (2024), lower middle market transactions generally close at 3x to 6x normalized EBITDA, with the specific multiple driven heavily by management stability and customer concentration.13 An error of $200,000 in the normalized figure—common when failing to account for the owner's multifaceted roles—translates to $600,000 to $1.2 million in lost enterprise value at a 3x to 6x multiple.13

## Academic Research and Peer-Reviewed Citations on Concentration Risk

Academic literature supports the practitioner-led conclusion that concentration risk—whether in the form of a "key man" or a single customer—mandates a significant valuation discount. Research published in the American Journal of Family Law and the CPA Journal emphasizes the "importance of separating the personal goodwill of the entrepreneur from that of the firm itself".24

Peer-reviewed studies by Nicholas Lynch in the CPA Journal (2013) found that for professional practices and SMEs, personal goodwill can account for 28% to 29% of the total value.26 His research suggests that without documented planning and "institutionalization" of the owner's reputation, this value is highly fragile during a transition. Further academic work on firm diversification by Denis, Denis, and Yost (2002) indicates that firms lacking industrial or managerial diversification trade at a valuation discount of 20% to 34%.27

Additionally, Master's level research by Masi Virta (2025) confirms the existence of the "private company discount," noting that the discount increases during times of tight liquidity—a condition often triggered for owner-dependent firms because lenders view them as riskier assets.28 Lending requirements for management buyouts (MBOs) typically require 1.25x to 1.5x debt service coverage; if owner dependency puts the business's cash flow predictability in doubt, lenders will reduce the available debt, which in turn reduces the purchase price a buyer can offer.29

## Impact on Deal Structure: The "Contingent Value" Discount

The discount applied to owner-dependent SMEs is not always reflected as a simple reduction in the headline purchase price; it is frequently embedded in the deal structure. When a buyer perceives high owner dependency, they shift the risk of the transition to the seller through contingent payments, such as earnouts or seller notes.3

According to the IBBA "Market Pulse" reports, in periods of economic uncertainty or high risk, there is a greater reliance on alternative financing options like mezzanine debt and seller financing.30 In a typical "clean" transaction, a seller might receive 84% of total consideration as cash at close.31 However, in deals characterized by high "founder dependency," buyers often require:

- **Extended Earnouts:** Requiring 2 to 3 years of post-closing involvement, with a significant portion of the price contingent on revenue retention.3
- **Larger Escrow Amounts:** Sellers may face larger holdbacks to cover potential relationship losses or operational failures following the owner's exit.3
- **Reduced Upfront Cash:** Founder-dependent firms may receive as little as 50% to 60% of the enterprise value as cash at close, with the remainder "at risk" in the form of a performance-based earnout.3

This "structural discount" means that even if the nominal sale price appears high, the present value of the deal is significantly lower due to the risk and time-value of the deferred payments.

## Institutional Preparedness and the "Value Gap"

The Exit Planning Institute (EPI) provides a unique perspective on the "Value Gap"—the difference between what a business is currently worth and what it could be worth if owner dependency were eliminated.32 The "2023 National State of Owner Readiness Report" indicates that 75% of owners plan to exit within the next decade, yet 59% have no written transition plan.32

EPI's "Attractiveness Index" uses a scoring system where a score of 50% or lower indicates that a company is considered "discounted" by the market and serves as a red flag for buyers.34 A score of 58% to 72% reflects above-average attractiveness, and only those above 72% are considered "best-in-class".34 For most owner-dependent SMEs, the scores fall into the "discounted" range, reflecting a failure to build "Structural Capital" (systems) and "Human Capital" (a management team).1

Research from BizBuySell's Insight Reports reinforces this, stating that "reducing owner dependence" is a critical step for increasing business value, as it allows potential buyers to "recognize the worth of the business and visualize themselves effortlessly assuming ownership".4 The BizBuySell "Buyer-Seller Confidence Index" highlights that while buyers remain confident, they are increasingly "focusing on high-quality companies with demonstrable, strong fundamentals"—a market shift that widens the discount for owner-dependent "Main Street" businesses.2

## Conclusions on the Financial Impact of Management Centralization

The data from both academic journals and practitioner benchmarks confirms that owner dependency is the primary "valuation killer" in the SME and lower middle market segments. For an enterprise with a transaction value between $500,000 and $10,000,000, the failure to institutionalize management and decouple the business from the persona of the founder results in a quantifiable reduction in value that typically ranges from 20% to 50%.

Sophisticated buyers, including private equity "search funds" and strategic acquirers, price this risk through multiple compression—moving a business from a "best-in-class" multiple (e.g., 6x EBITDA) to a "distressed or owner-centric" multiple (e.g., 3x-4x EBITDA). This reduction is not merely a negotiation tactic but is rooted in the increased cost of capital, the requirement for higher debt service coverage by lenders, and the mathematical allocation of goodwill to non-transferable personal attributes through models like MUM.

Furthermore, the discount is often "hidden" in deal terms, with high-dependency businesses facing larger earnouts and reduced cash at close, effectively discounting the present value of the transaction further. To achieve a premium valuation, an SME must demonstrate that its cash flows are a product of its structural and human capital—systems and teams—rather than the "inspiration and perspiration" of a single owner. The quantitative evidence suggests that "de-risking" the owner's role is the most effective lever for value creation, potentially adding $1.00 of enterprise value for every $1.00 of transferable profit institutionalized.
